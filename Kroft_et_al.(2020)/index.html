Imperfect Competition and Rents in Labor and Product Markets: The Case of the Construction Industry

Posted on 2020-06-09
Kory Kroft, Yao Luo, Magne Mogstad and Bradley Setzler (2020, Working Paper)

Availability

The paper is available at Yao Luo's website, and is presented by Yao Luo at 2020 SUFE-IESR IO workshop (06/09/2020).

Summary

The paper focuses on examining the existence and effect of labor market power. The authors utilize the self-collected data on DOT procurement auctions on construction projects, merged with U.S. Employer-Employee data from 2001 to 2015.

The main idea is that, winning the auction gives the winners an edge on labor market power which later translates into higher revenue in private market.

The authors focus on firms participating in the 2010 auctions who never won before. By comparing the performance after 2010 of winners (treatment group) and losers (control group), the authors find a significant positive gap emerging afterwards in terms of both firm outcomes and worker outcomes in a reduced form estimation.

As for structural estimation, the authors set up a three-stage model:

  1. The firms are involved in the procurement auction with a random TFP;
  2. Depending on winning or losing the auction, the firms hire labor and rent capitals;
  3. The firms realize a revenue from private market and pay the winning bid if necessary.

The key parameters to be estimated from the structural form are \(\theta\) (for labor market power) and \(\rho\) (for product market power). After estimating the parameters using GMM, the authors are able to decompose the rent (markup charged by firms) into different sources: winning the auction, labor market power and product market power.

Comments

  • There are no data on capital expenditure for firms, and thus the authors assume that all capitals are rented at an interest rate \(r\) which needs to be estimated as well.
  • In the model the entry into industry and entry into auction are both exogeneous and sunk. In other words, the auction is a free lottery ticket for the firms. However, in reality, both entry decisions are endogenous, and at some costs as well. The positive markup might be the payoff of paying the entry cost in the first place.

Related Literature

  • The empirical design is similar to Greenstone and Moretti(2004) "Million Dollar Plant"
  • Another recent paper on labor market power is Charles, Hurst and Notowidigdo(2017, NBER)
