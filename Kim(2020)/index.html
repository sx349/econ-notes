Informed Agent's Advice: Theory and Evidence

Posted on 2020-06-27
Sanghoon Kim (2020, Working Paper)

Availability

The paper is available at Sanghoon Kim's website, and will be presented by Sanghoon Kim at 2020 WEAI virtual conference (06/29/2020).

Summary

The paper examines the role of an informed agent in the bilateral bargaining game from Chatterjee and Samuelson (1983). More specifically, the author focuses on the real estate market and examine how brokers' different objective (trade-maximizing v.s. price-maximizing) would change the effect. The author uses data from the South Korea real estate market to empricially test the theory, utilizing the fact that the maximum limits on commissions according to housing prices essentially provides different compensation schemes for brokers.

The model consists of three players: a buyer, a seller and a broker as the informed agent. The buyer knows exactly her own valuation, but only knows a distribution of the seller's valuation, and vice versa. The broker, on the other hand, with a pre-determined partition of the valuation space, knows on which interval the valuations lie in but not the actual valuations. Then the broker decides whether to report the interval of one side truthfully to the other side, depending on the objective.

Assumptions 1 and 2 assume that the density of valuation distribution and partition of the valuation space are both reflection-symmetric, i.e., the density / partition for the buyer side is a mirrored copy of that for the seller side.

Lemma 1 summarizes the last stage equilibrium from Chatterjee and Samuelson (1983), where the buyer and the seller are assumed to have common knowledge on the joint distribution. Generally, if trade is guaranteed, the buyer and seller can coordinate on any in-between prices which leads to multiple equilibria; if trade is never going to happen, the buyer (seller) may price extremely low (high); otherwise, there is a unique bidding function determined by FOC.

Lemma 2 shows that if opponent's valuation falls in a higher interval, one's equilibrium offer would be higher.

Lemma 3 discusses when the informed agent deviates from reporting truthfully. Misreporting the buyer's (seller's) valuation upward (downward) makes the seller (buyer) believes the trade is easier to happen, and thus is less likely to make no-trade offer, and bid more aggressively, and vice versa.

Theorem 1 is the major result of the paper. The author proves that whenever a trade-maximizing agent would deviate, so would the price-maximizing agent. Therefore, when there exists a truthful reporting equilibrium for price-maximizing agent, there also exists one for trade-maximizing agent.

Lemma 4 and Lemma 5 compares the truthful reporting equilibrium with the babbling equilibrium (the informed agent report nothing). The former raises the probability of trade as well as the expected sales prices.

Combining Theorem 1 and Lemma 4 and 5, Corollary 2 and 3 suggests that a trade-maximizing agent gets higher probability of trade as well as higher expected sales prices than the price-maximizing agent. Intuitively, this is similar to the no commitment problem: the price-maximizing agent cannot mimic the trade-maximizing agent since it will deviate from truthful reporting, and thus in general the expected sales price is lower.

The author uses South Korea real estate transactions from 2006 to 2017 (all transactions must be reported to the Ministry of Land, Infrasctructure and Transport, and the Ministry provides an API for public access) to empirically test the theoretical findings. To separate agents according to their objectives, the author utilizes the commission regulation effective across the country. For houses with sales price below 50 million Won, the commssion rate is 0.6 % with a maximum limit of 0.25 million Won. Therefore, agents get a constant 0.25 million Won when matchmaking for a house sold between 41.6 million and 50 million. Similarly, agents get a constant 0.8 million won when the house price falls between 160 and 200 million Won.

There is one more problem with the data: it only contains sales data but not listing, and thus the author cannot calculate the actual probability of trading. The author uses a hierachical cluster analysis (an unsupervised machine learning method) to group the sales and assume that listing remains constant within each group, and thus the number of sales is proportional to the probability of trading.

Using fixed effect regression on the constructed unbalanced panel data, the author finds that the probability of trading for a trade-maximizing agent is 0.22-0.23% higher than that for a price-maximizing agent.

Comments

  • The multiple equilibria issue when the lower bound of buyer's valuation is higher than the upper bound of seller's valuation is not addressed.
  • Assumptions 1 and 2 are too critical for theorem 1 to hold. In the proof of Theorem 1, whenever the trade-maximizing informed agent has an incentive to under-report the valuation for one side or both, due to Assumptions 1 and 2, the trade-maximizing informed agent always has an incentive to over-report the valuation in a mirrored situation, and thus the price-maximizing informed agent would deviate as well. In other words, without Assumptions 1 and 2, we can easily imagine that, sometimes when the trade-maximizing informed agent has an incentive to under-report, the price-maximizing agent would not mimic it due to that under-reporting may reduce the sales price when trade actually takes place. Therefore, the author is obliged to provide further evidence to suppport these assumptions.
  • There might be other equilibria that lies between truthful reporting and babbling. Note that in terms of partitioning, truthful reporting is the finest possible strucutre while babbling is the coarsest. In equilibrium, the informed agent might perefer grouping some intervals. The result may still hold under such a possibility but then a delicate equilibrium selection rule is required when truthful equilibrium is not available, i.e., the babbling equilibrium will be chosen but not the others.
  • For the empirical exercise, what if the possible range of valuation of a certain unit is too wide such that it covers both the intervals where the agent gets fixed commission and those where the agent gets percentage commission? Since we only observe data from the final sales but not the offers or the actual valuation, i.e., only the ex-post prices, there is no guarantee that the agent has ex-ante chosen to be trade-maximizing or price-maximizing, or possibly a hybrid.

Related Literature

  • Myerson and Satterthwaite (1983) provides a negative theorem on the desired properties for games with asymmetric information: there is no mechanism that satisfies all of ex-ante IR, IC, weakly balanced budget, and ex-post efficiency.
  • Chatterjee and Samuelson (1983) looks specifically into bilateral bargaining games with one buyer and one seller, and shows that it's indeed ex-post inefficient.
  • Farrell and Gibbons (1989) introduces cheap talk into the bilateral bargaining game.
  • Suvorov and Tsybuleva (2010) considers the role of intermediary in the bilateral bargaining game, but assume uniform distribution for either side.
  • Zietz and Sirmans (2011) provides an extensive review of literature of real estate brokerage in the 2000s.
